Brief Introduction

Abstract. Scene reconstruction from sparse-view satellite images remains a challenging task due to insufficient geometric constraints. While generalizable 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) methods have shown high efficiency for novel-view synthesis, existing methods are often unsuitable for satellite imagery due to incompatibility with the rational polynomial coefficient (RPC) model and limited generalization capability. In this work, we present SkySplat++, a hierarchical generalizable 3DGS framework that extends our previous SkySplat to further improve scene reconstruction. SkySplat++ refines Gaussian representations in a coarse-to-fine manner, enabling robust modeling of large-scale scene structures and fine-grained texture details. A Height-Guided Hierarchical Cost Volume Construction (HG-HCV) module is integrated to progressively narrow the height search range, facilitating more accurate cost volume construction, and a hybrid height supervision combining relative and absolute signals to improve geometric accuracy. Compared to per-scene optimization methods, SkySplat++ achieves up to 1070 times speed up over EOGS with higher accuracy. It also outperforms generalizable 3DGS baselines, achieving a 5–10 m reduction in MAE on two benchmarks and demonstrating strong cross-dataset generalization on the MVS3D test set.

We propose SkySplat++, a self-supervised and generalizable framework that avoids per-scene optimization. First, multi-view features are extracted from the sparse input images, which are then used to construct hierarchical cost volumes through RPC warping, providing robust geometric cues under sparse-view conditions. These cues are subsequently leveraged to estimate hierarchical Gaussian parameters, yielding an expressive 3D representation. Finally, a multi-view consistency aggregation strategy is applied to refine the reconstructed scene. During training, multi-view photometric self-supervision and a hybrid height supervision are jointly employed to stabilize height estimation under radiometric inconsistencies.
